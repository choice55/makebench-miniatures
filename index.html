The Method

High-Tech Meets
Hand-Craft

Every MakeBench piece starts on a workbench in Catonsville, Maryland. Where it ends up is somewhere between history and imagination. The studio does not choose between traditional craft and modern technology. It uses both, fully.

Each plank of miniature hardwood flooring is cut, stained, and laid by hand. Each architectural component is engineered in CAD and printed with micron-level precision. Each light source is wired, coded, and calibrated to tell the right story at the right hour.

  • 3D Print FLASHFORGE Adventurer 5M Pro for custom architectural components, furniture forms, and structural elements
  • Artisan Balsa and basswood construction, professional airbrushing, hand-applied weathering and patina
  • Electronics Custom-coded LED matrices, functional miniature displays, and embedded smart lighting systems
  • Scale 1:12 — one inch represents one foot — the language of serious miniature craft
The Studio
"The goal is never just to replicate a room. It is to capture a moment, a feeling, a fragment of time that deserves to be kept."

MakeBench Miniatures started in 2020 on a single workbench in Catonsville, Maryland, during the quietest stretch of the pandemic. What began as a way to stay focused and make something real has grown into a full creative practice, one that takes history, personal memory, and technology seriously at 1:12 scale.

The studio is Aaron Graham's. He is a non-practicing attorney and higher education professional who has been building miniatures long enough to have a system and an aesthetic. The Fragments of Family Folklore series is the most personal part of the work: each piece preserves a real story, a real memory, in a form you can hold and look at up close.

The studio is rooted in the Maryland maker community, and the work reflects that: hands-on, patient, and built to last. The belief driving all of it is simple enough. With the right tools and enough care, you can fit something true into the palm of your hand.
